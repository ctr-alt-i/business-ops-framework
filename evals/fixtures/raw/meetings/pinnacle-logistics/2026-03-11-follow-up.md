Participants:
- Bryan Mendez (CTRL.ALT.I, Solution Lead)

- Patricia Walsh (Pinnacle Logistics, CFO)

Sarah Chen: Patricia, this is Bryan from CTRL.ALT.I. We had a great initial conversation last week and I wanted you in the room before we go further.

Patricia Walsh: Appreciate it. Bryan, I've read Sarah's recap. Two things on my mind: ROI and risk. Walk me through both.

Bryan Mendez: Sure. On ROI - Sarah mentioned you're missing early-payment discounts. If you're seeing even 1% discount capture on, call it, $400K of monthly invoice volume that qualifies, that's $48K a year. The build pays for itself in roughly six months on discount capture alone, before we even talk about labor reallocation.

Patricia Walsh: That math assumes we actually capture the discounts. What's the failure mode?

Bryan Mendez: Fair. The two failure modes are: extraction errors that send wrong data to QuickBooks, and approval bottlenecks. We solve the first with the confidence threshold and human review queue. The second one - that's actually something I want to raise. Right now your approval flow happens after the invoice is in QuickBooks, which means even if we automate ingestion, you're still bottlenecked on someone clicking approve.

Patricia Walsh: That's accurate. It's me and one controller doing approvals.

Bryan Mendez: I'd recommend we add a Slack-based approval workflow. Invoice gets extracted, a Slack message goes to the right approver with the PDF preview and a one-click approve or reject. Approved invoices flow into QuickBooks automatically. Rejected ones come back to the queue with a comment.

Patricia Walsh: I like that. What does it do to the price?

Bryan Mendez: It adds about $3,000 to the build and another $300 a month. So we'd be looking at roughly $24,000 build, $1,800 monthly.

Patricia Walsh: Hm. And what's your confidence on the timeline?

Bryan Mendez: With Slack added, I'd push it from six to eight weeks. I'd rather give you a realistic number than miss a deadline.

Patricia Walsh: We need this live before our fiscal year close on June 30. Can you commit to that?

Bryan Mendez: If we kick off by April 1, yes. Comfortable buffer.

Sarah Chen: One thing - let's drop GoHighLevel from any of this paperwork. That's a separate conversation for Q3.

Bryan Mendez: Already off the table. Last thing from my side - I want to flag that we'd be using N8N as the orchestration layer. It's hosted, you'd own the workflows, and if you ever wanted to bring this in-house, the handoff is clean. I mention it because some clients have opinions about that.

Patricia Walsh: No opinion as long as it works and we're not locked in.

Bryan Mendez: You're not. Let me put the proposal together. I'll have it to you by end of next week.

Patricia Walsh: Send it to Sarah and me both. And Bryan - if there's a way to phase it so we can see something working in the first three weeks, that would help me sleep.

Bryan Mendez: I'll structure it that way. Phase 1 ingestion-only, Phase 2 approval workflow, Phase 3 production cutover.
